Introduction

In today’s ever-growing digital era, what began as a tool of convenience, smartphones and devices have become a vital piece of our daily life. While these items enable instant communication across the globe, growing usage has evolved into an increasing psychological and social crisis: smartphone addiction. Characterized by the compulsive need to check notifications and apps, withdrawal symptoms, and severe interference with daily life, smartphone dependency shows an increasing link to rising rates of anxiety, sleep deprivation, and diminished academic performance among young adults [@lin2014]. Identifying the precise behavioral, social, and psychological drivers of this addiction is critical for developing effective interventions. Available data tracking screen time, app usage, and psychological metrics frequently contain dozens of interconnected variables. Traditional linear regression models struggle under this weight, frequently from overfitting and multicollinearity, which obscures the true narrative hidden within the data [@james2013].

In this project, we will explore the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) method, commonly known as LASSO regression, to isolate the most critical predictors of smartphone addiction. Introduced by Robert Tibshirani [-@tibshirani1996], LASSO is a powerful statistical method designed for both regularization and variable selection. Traditional ordinary least squares (OLS) regression treats every available variable as important, which often introduces noise and reduces predictive accuracy when dealing with large datasets. LASSO solves this problem by adding a mathematical constraint—known as an L1 penalty—to the sum of the absolute values of the regression coefficients [@tibshirani1996].

This L1 penalty performs a unique function: it actively shrinks the coefficients of less important variables toward zero [@james2013]. Notably, unlike other methods such as Ridge regression, LASSO can force coefficients to become exactly zero [@tibshirani1996]. LASSO automatically performs variable selection, completely removing irrelevant or redundant features from the final model, resulting in a highly interpretable, sparse model consisting only of the most statistically significant predictors.

Our dataset explores problematic smartphone usage from survey data collected in China using a Computer Adaptive Testing (CAT) item bank [@gao2024]. It is comprised of 1,619 respondents with complete data on six instrument families plus the demographics.

Here we will apply LASSO regression to a comprehensive smartphone addiction dataset to break down which specific behaviors and demographics truly drive the dependencies. Smartphone datasets are particularly noisy, with variables like social media screen time, daily notification counts, and self-reported anxiety scores often overlapping and distorting traditional models. By implementing LASSO, we can mathematically weed out any statistical noise, isolating the leading factors [@james2013]. Ideally, this approach will aid us in answering a crucial question: with the countless digital metrics tracked today, which factors truly lead to smartphone addictions? Our findings aim to provide a streamlined, data-driven framework that peers and clinicians alike can use to understand and combat digital dependency.

Methods

We model smartphone addiction severity with penalized linear regression. Each respondent contributes item-level responses from six validated instrument families (NMP, SAPS, SPAI, MPAS, MPATS, SAS) plus demographics. After listwise deletion of incomplete records, \(n = 1{,}619\) complete cases remain. The primary continuous outcome is SPAI total, the sum of 20 SPAI items (range 20–80) [@lin2014]. SPAI items are excluded from the predictor set when predicting SPAI total to avoid circularity; we instead ask whether nomophobia, proneness, and related scales track overall SPAI severity. A secondary categorical outcome, SPAI item 12, captures perceived negative impact on study or work on a four-point Likert scale.

Ordinary least squares and why LASSO

A standard linear model for continuous outcome \(y_i\) and predictors \(\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^p\) is

\[y_i = \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \beta_j x_{ij} + \varepsilon_i, \qquad i = 1,\ldots,n. \tag{1}\]

Ordinary least squares (OLS) minimizes the residual sum of squares \(\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\). With dozens of correlated survey items, OLS tends to overfit, inflate coefficient variance, and produce unstable signs under multicollinearity [@james2013; @mcneish2015]. Ridge regression adds an \(L_2\) penalty that shrinks coefficients but rarely sets them exactly to zero [@hoerl1970]. LASSO replaces that penalty with an \(L_1\) term, enabling simultaneous shrinkage and variable selection [@tibshirani1996; @hesterberg2008].

LASSO objective and tuning

LASSO estimates coefficients by minimizing the penalized least-squares objective [@tibshirani1996; @santosa1986]:

\[\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j x_{ij}\right)^2 + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^{p}|\beta_j| \right\}. \tag{2}\]

The tuning parameter \(\lambda \geq 0\) controls the bias–variance trade-off. When \(\lambda = 0\), Equation (2) reduces to OLS; as \(\lambda\) increases, more coefficients are driven to exactly zero, yielding a sparse, interpretable model [@efron2004; @dalalyan2017]. We select \(\lambda\) with 10-fold cross-validation on an 80/20 train–test split, reporting both lambda.min (lowest CV error) and lambda.1se (simplest model within one standard error of the minimum) [@james2013]. For the main paper result we emphasize plain LASSO at lambda.1se as a deliberate parsimony choice: it retains nearly the same test \(R^2\) while selecting far fewer predictors.

Test-set performance for continuous models is summarized by

\[R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i}(y_i - \bar{y})^2}, \qquad \mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}. \tag{3}\]

Plain LASSO versus group LASSO

We fit two complementary LASSO variants implemented in glmnet (plain) and grpreg (group) [@james2013; @huang2024].

Plain LASSO (alpha = 1, family = "gaussian") performs item-level selection: any individual survey item may enter or leave the model. Group LASSO assigns all items within a scale (e.g., SAPS) to a single group and selects entire scales at once [@yuan2006; @huang2024]. Plain LASSO answers which specific items matter; group LASSO answers which whole instruments matter when scales are treated as coherent units.

For classification of SPAI item 12, we additionally fit multinomial LASSO (family = "multinomial") and refit a multinomial log-linear model on the selected predictors (post-LASSO) to improve class probabilities [@james2013]. Class imbalance in SPAI item 12 is evaluated with balanced accuracy and Cohen’s \(\kappa\) on the held-out test set.

Justification for this problem and data

Our CAT item bank contains on the order of 100 item-level predictors that measure overlapping facets of problematic phone use [@gao2024]. Pearson correlations among scale totals exceed \(r = 0.64\) for several pairs (see Data Exploration), so multicollinearity is expected rather than exceptional. LASSO is well suited to high-dimensional, collinear psychological survey data because it stabilizes estimation and returns a sparse subset of items or scales that best predict addiction severity [@mcneish2015; @dalalyan2017]. Comparing plain and group LASSO, alongside lambda.min and lambda.1se, lets us balance predictive accuracy against interpretability—an important consideration when communicating results to clinicians and educators [@james2013; @huang2024].

Analysis and Results

Data Exploration and Visualization

Data come from a Computer Adaptive Testing (CAT) item bank on problematic mobile phone use [@gao2024]. Respondents completed items from six instrument families—nomophobia (NMP), smartphone addiction proneness (SAPS), the Smartphone Addiction Inventory (SPAI) [@lin2014], the Mobile Phone Addiction Scale (MPAS), the Mobile Phone Addiction Tendency Scale (MPATS), and the Smartphone Addiction Scale (SAS-C and SAS-CA)—along with demographics (age, gender, grade). The raw file contains \(n = 1{,}619\) respondents with complete records on all variables; no listwise missingness remains after filtering. Scale totals are computed as the sum of item scores within each instrument (higher totals indicate more problematic use, per the item bank codebook).

The sample is predominantly young adults (mean age \(= 18.8\) years, \(SD = 2.9\)), with more female (\(n = 991\)) than male (\(n = 628\)) respondents. SPAI total—the primary outcome for regression—has mean \(= 41.8\) (\(SD = 10.2\)) on the 20–80 scale, with a roughly symmetric distribution centered below the midpoint.

Modeling and Results

Data preprocessing

We began with the raw CAT item bank file (\(n = 1{,}619\) rows, 101 variables) and retained only complete cases; no listwise missingness required exclusion. The primary outcome SPAI total was computed as the sum of 20 SPAI items. All other SPAI item columns were removed from the predictor matrix when modeling SPAI total to avoid circularity. Demographics (age, gender, grade) and item-level responses from NMP, SAPS, MPAS, MPATS, and SAS (SAS-C and SAS-CA) entered as predictors. Gender and grade were coded as factors; all item responses were treated as numeric. Predictors were assembled with a model matrix (no intercept column in the penalized fit). Respondents were split 80/20 into training and test sets using stratification on SPAI item 12 (caret::createDataPartition) so that the imbalanced categorical outcome remained represented in both partitions; all continuous models below use the same split.

LASSO regression for SPAI total

We fit plain LASSO (glmnet, alpha = 1, Gaussian family) and group LASSO (grpreg, scales as groups) on the training set with 10-fold cross-validation to select \(\lambda\). Test-set performance is reported with \(R^2\) and RMSE (Equation 3). Following our Methods section, we emphasize plain LASSO at lambda.1se as the recommended model: it achieves strong predictive accuracy while retaining far fewer predictors than group LASSO at comparable \(R^2\) [@james2013; @huang2024].

library(glmnet)
library(grpreg)
library(caret)

RANDOM_SEED <- 123
set.seed(RANDOM_SEED)

assign_group <- function(nm) {
  if (grepl("^gender", nm)) return("gender")
  if (grepl("^grade", nm)) return("grade")
  if (nm == "age") return("age")
  if (grepl("^NMP_", nm)) return("NMP")
  if (grepl("^SAPS_", nm)) return("SAPS")
  if (grepl("^SPAI_", nm)) return("SPAI")
  if (grepl("^MPAS_", nm)) return("MPAS")
  if (grepl("^MPATS", nm)) return("MPATS")
  if (grepl("^SAS_CA", nm)) return("SAS_CA")
  if (grepl("^SAS_C", nm)) return("SAS_C")
  "other"
}

make_reg_mm <- function(dat) {
  spai_items <- paste0("SPAI_", 1:20)
  d <- dat[, !names(dat) %in% c(spai_items, "SPAI_total"), drop = FALSE]
  d$SPAI_total <- dat$SPAI_total
  x <- model.matrix(SPAI_total ~ ., data = d)[, -1]
  list(
    x = x,
    y = d$SPAI_total,
    group = as.numeric(factor(sapply(colnames(x), assign_group)))
  )
}

plain_lasso_vars <- function(cv_fit, lambda_label, cols) {
  raw_coef <- coef(cv_fit, s = lambda_label)
  mat <- as.matrix(raw_coef)
  vars <- rownames(mat)[mat[, 1] != 0]
  intersect(setdiff(vars, "(Intercept)"), cols)
}

group_lasso_vars <- function(cv_fit, lambda_val, cols) {
  coefs <- coef(cv_fit, lambda = lambda_val)
  intersect(names(coefs)[coefs != 0 & names(coefs) != "(Intercept)"], cols)
}

reg_metrics <- function(y_true, y_pred) {
  ss_res <- sum((y_true - y_pred)^2)
  ss_tot <- sum((y_true - mean(y_true))^2)
  list(
    R2 = 1 - ss_res / ss_tot,
    RMSE = sqrt(mean((y_true - y_pred)^2))
  )
}

mod_dat <- dat
mod_dat$gender <- as.factor(mod_dat$gender)
mod_dat$grade <- as.factor(mod_dat$grade)
mod_dat$SPAI_12 <- as.factor(mod_dat$SPAI_12)

train_index <- createDataPartition(mod_dat$SPAI_12, p = 0.80, list = FALSE)
mm_reg <- make_reg_mm(mod_dat)
xtr <- mm_reg$x[train_index, , drop = FALSE]
xte <- mm_reg$x[-train_index, , drop = FALSE]
ytr <- mm_reg$y[train_index]
yte <- mm_reg$y[-train_index]

cv_plain <- cv.glmnet(xtr, ytr, alpha = 1, family = "gaussian", nfolds = 10)
pred_plain <- as.numeric(predict(cv_plain, xte, s = "lambda.1se"))
met_plain <- reg_metrics(yte, pred_plain)
sel_plain <- plain_lasso_vars(cv_plain, "lambda.1se", colnames(mm_reg$x))

cv_grp <- cv.grpreg(xtr, ytr, mm_reg$group, family = "gaussian", nfolds = 10)
lam_grp <- cv_grp$lambda.min
pred_grp <- as.numeric(predict(cv_grp, xte, lambda = lam_grp))
met_grp <- reg_metrics(yte, pred_grp)
sel_grp <- group_lasso_vars(cv_grp, lam_grp, colnames(mm_reg$x))

gauss_results <- data.frame(
  Model = c("Plain LASSO | lambda.1se", "Group LASSO | lambda.min"),
  R2 = round(c(met_plain$R2, met_grp$R2), 3),
  RMSE = round(c(met_plain$RMSE, met_grp$RMSE), 2),
  N_selected = c(length(sel_plain), length(sel_grp))
)

scale_counts <- sel_plain %>%
  sapply(assign_group) %>%
  table() %>%
  as.data.frame(stringsAsFactors = FALSE)
names(scale_counts) <- c("Scale", "N_items")

On the held-out test set (\(n = 322\)), plain LASSO at lambda.1se achieved \(R^2 = 0.746\) and RMSE \(= 5.3\), selecting 22 predictors. Group LASSO at lambda.min reached a similar \(R^2 = 0.762\) but retained 98 predictors—substantially more predictors—substantially more complexity for little additional explanatory gain. In practical terms, the non-SPAI scales explain roughly three-quarters of the variation in SPAI total severity on respondents the model had not seen during training.

Item-level plain LASSO selection was concentrated in SAPS (addiction proneness) and SAS (cognitive and behavioral addiction symptoms). These results align with the exploratory correlation structure: SAS and SAPS track SPAI total most closely, and LASSO uses that shared variance efficiently while zeroing out redundant nomophobia items. The high test \(R^2\) should be interpreted as convergent validity among overlapping addiction measures rather than evidence of causal effects [@gao2024; @lin2014].

Conclusion

Using LASSO regression on a Chinese CAT item bank of problematic mobile phone use [@gao2024], we showed that a sparse plain-LASSO model can predict SPAI total addiction severity with strong held-out accuracy (\(R^2 = 0.75\)) from other validated scales and demographics. SAPS and SAS emerged as the dominant predictors; NMP (nomophobia) added little once those instruments were in the model. Plain LASSO at lambda.1se offered the best balance of fit and parsimony relative to group LASSO, which selected many more items for comparable performance.

Several implications follow from these findings. First, the instruments in this item bank measure closely related facets of problematic smartphone use; high predictive overlap supports their use as a coherent battery but also underscores the multicollinearity that motivates penalized regression over ordinary least squares [@james2013; @mcneish2015]. Second, because the sample was collected in China from predominantly young adults (mean age \(= 18.8\) years), results should not be generalized uncritically to other countries or age groups without external validation [@gao2024]. Cultural norms around mobile use, educational pressure, and access to digital services differ across settings; replication in U.S. or other populations would clarify whether SAPS and SAS remain the leading predictors of SPAI severity. Third, for clinicians and educators working with similar screening batteries, a short subset of SAPS and SAS items identified by LASSO may suffice to estimate overall severity, though prospective validation is still needed.

Future work could examine post-LASSO refitting, robustness across train–test splits and random seeds, and ordered-outcome models for SPAI item 12. Extending the analysis to new geographic samples would strengthen claims about which behavioral and psychological drivers of smartphone addiction are universal versus context-specific.
